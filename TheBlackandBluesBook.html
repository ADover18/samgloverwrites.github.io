THE BLACK AND BLUES BOOK

It was said even Charlie, Dan, and the rest of them fell prey to the black and blues So and so had gone up north,

and no one could get a hold of grass, though there was coke in every public bathroom from Glasgow to Shetland.

No one had the time for even coffee or for a half of whatever, any longer.

A certain thickening of the blood and of the air was observed as light bent out of shape, it did not seem to move as it was once want to, some years ago.

That who-did-you-know got concrete feet, and when I wished upon a teevee satellite some evening, a quarter to witching hour, I was answered the following morning by a jovial JML presenter, who, propped upon my television stand, announced candidly it might never happen, but when it did, it would happen sort of sideways, and I wouldn't know the difference anyway.

There were whisperings and malingerings around known locations, much like whispers while the walls crawl. And when they quit the crawling, they slowed to a stop, rolled into the by-lane and the engine cut, then nothing but red glare of hazards in the middle of the night.

There were no places called home any longer, for anyone, and we all sort of slid by one another and tipped our caps. And goddamn you if you didn't know the handshake because we know now, you were never one of us. Our legs got stripped of all fat and nothing but sinew, nothing but muscle on us, and a thin coat for the winter, because we were all on our minimum wage feet, all the time. And you could say nothing to us which could offend.

And you could do nothing to us that would hurt.

And there was very little to account for in the bank.

And sometimes you would turn around and look to find another week or month had passed by too quick to see, you'd been living so fast, so fast you were living a second at a time, splitting moments into their constituent parts like atoms.

Well,

it was around the time all this was happening that some guy figured he'd talk about himself for a minute. To say we were friends would say too much or too little, but we dug each-other's minds in the strange way you sometimes happen to do. We'd known each-other by face - though not name - when, one chill November afternoon, I found him passed out in a bar stool with a notebook in his

hand. My attempts to make meaningful contact went unnoticed, as he was frightfully pissed. We hadn't seen each-other in some years though, and I was interested in where he'd been; the murder squads had wiped out most of the undesirables and the creatives some years ago. Not that I need to remind you, but we inhabit more enlightened times now, don't we?

In any case, with the possibility for conversation minimal, I took the notebook from his hands and read what he had to say. While I didn't find much in particular you might call likeable, it resonated with me the way certain sounds do, like a far-off train. I was still sitting next to him, reading, when he awoke some hours later, the bar festooned with empty schooners, and myself rather the worse for wear. After he had hurriedly reclaimed the text, he and I traded battle scars and raps, and we continued to meet on irregular occasions throughout the remaining years of his life. The conversations we had, though I never truly appreciated them at the time, rank amongst the most intriguing I've enjoyed in the quiet years since my retreat from the scene.

Despite the candour we enjoyed in these meetings, one subject we never discussed was the notebook I'd read that day in the bar. I suppose much of it expounded on experiences too particular to him to be of much interest to anyone else. Despite the literary ambitions of his younger, less inebriated years, I think that by the time of writing, writing itself had become for him more of an evacuation, and an emptiness, than a means of communication. That said, there was always something in him

which seemed to spite this, a voice that wanted thrown in with other people's, and this trace element, I expect, is what resonated within me all those years ago. A sort of ghost in the shell.

The manuscript to which I refer, and which I here preface, bore more resemblance to an extended face-book post than a work of literary significance (or otherwise). Regardless, I couldn't help but find a certain quality in what he said, haggard and dissolute though it may be. I suppose that says more about myself and he, the times we lived through, than it does about the work itself. But now

that he's gone wherever people go, and has taken the tonsure one last time, before one last walk down that narrow road, I thought I might post his narcissistic rag and see if anyone felt the same as I did - as I still do - about what he wrote.

Anyway, here he is, in his own words; singing drunk in the shower, tripping over skips in the vinyl, whilst, high, the world rolls by, or even, yes, perhaps even stops for him - now and again.
